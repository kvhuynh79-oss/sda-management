5. Maintenance response records -- Reactive maintenance requests need timestamps: when reported, when actioned, when resolved. Preventative schedules need evidence of completion.

The common thread? Evidence with timestamps. Not "we do this" -- but "here is the dated proof we did this."

### Tuesday -- Compliance Tips
**Headline**: NDIS incident reporting: the 24-hour rule that catches providers off guard

**Post**:
Under the NDIS (Incident Management and Reportable Incidents) Rules 2018, registered providers must notify the NDIS Commission of reportable incidents within 24 hours of becoming aware of them.

Not 24 business hours. Not "by close of business." Twenty-four hours from awareness, including weekends and public holidays.

Reportable incidents include:
- The death of a person with disability
- Serious injury
- Abuse or neglect (including allegations)
- Unlawful sexual or physical contact
- Unauthorised use of restrictive practices

A 5-day written report must then follow with full details.

Here is where SDA providers get caught:

Scenario: A support worker reports a minor injury on Friday afternoon. The property manager reads the email on Monday morning. The Commission considers the provider to have been "aware" when the support worker reported it -- Friday afternoon. By Monday morning, 64 hours have elapsed. That is a breach.

Three things you can do right now:

1. Ensure incident reports can be submitted 24/7 -- not just during office hours. If your reporting system requires logging into a desktop application, your field staff cannot report from site at 9pm on a Saturday.

2. Set up instant notifications. When an incident is logged, the responsible person needs to know immediately -- push notification, SMS, or email.

3. Classify incidents at the point of entry. Your reporting form should ask: "Is this reportable to the NDIS Commission?" If yes, the 24-hour clock starts now.

### Tuesday -- Compliance Tips
**Headline**: APP 3 participant consent: what most SDA providers get wrong

**Post**:
Under Australian Privacy Principle 3, organisations must collect personal information by lawful and fair means, and only with the individual's consent. For NDIS participants, this is not a one-time checkbox.

Here is what APP 3 actually requires for SDA providers:

1. Informed consent. The participant (or their nominee) must understand what information you collect, why you collect it, who you share it with, and how they can access or correct it. Handing someone a 12-page privacy policy and asking them to sign does not meet the "informed" standard.

2. Capacity-appropriate communication. Many SDA participants have cognitive or communication disabilities. The NDIS Commission recommends Easy Read formats -- simplified language, visual supports, and clear structure. A standard consent form may not be accessible.

3. Consent has a shelf life. Best practice is annual renewal. If a participant gave consent 3 years ago and their circumstances have changed (new support coordinator, new plan, new living arrangement), that original consent may no longer cover your current data practices.

4. Withdrawal must be actionable. If a participant withdraws consent, you need a process for archiving their sensitive data while maintaining the records you are legally required to keep (tenancy records, incident history).

5. Evidence of consent. An auditor will not accept "they said yes on the phone." You need a dated, signed (or equivalent) consent record linked to the specific participant.

**Headline**: Your SDA participant data deserves bank-level encryption. Here is why.

**Post**:
SDA providers hold some of the most sensitive personal information in the Australian disability sector:

- NDIS participant numbers
- Dates of birth
- Medical and disability information
- Emergency contact details
- Financial information (bank accounts for rent contributions)
- Incident details (including allegations of abuse)

Under the Privacy Act 1988 and the Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme, if this information is accessed without authorisation, you have 72 hours to notify the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner and every affected individual.

The question is not whether you store this data. You have to. The question is how you protect it.

Three levels of data protection every SDA provider should implement:

Level 1: Access control. Not everyone on your team needs access to every participant's NDIS number. Role-based permissions ensure that staff only see the data relevant to their role.

Level 2: Encryption in transit. Any data moving between your device and your server should be encrypted. This is HTTPS -- most modern platforms do this by default.

Level 3: Encryption at rest. This is where most providers fall short. Even if someone gains access to your database, encrypted data at rest is unreadable without the encryption key. MySDAManager uses AES-256 encryption (the same standard used by banks and government agencies) for sensitive fields: NDIS numbers, dates of birth, emergency contacts, bank account numbers, and incident descriptions.

Plus HMAC-SHA256 blind indexes that allow you to search encrypted fields without decrypting the entire database.

### Tuesday -- Compliance Tips
**Headline**: Complaints handling: the 5-day acknowledgement window that most providers miss

**Post**:
Under the NDIS Practice Standards, providers must have an accessible complaints management system. The standard includes specific timeframes:

- Acknowledgement: 5 business days from receipt
- Resolution: Target of 21 business days
- Complex complaints: Extended timeline with documented justification

Here is what goes wrong:

A participant's family member sends an email complaint on a Wednesday. The property manager reads it Thursday but does not formally acknowledge it until the following Monday. By then, 3 business days have passed. If the formal acknowledgement letter is not sent until Wednesday (a week later), the provider has missed the 5-day window.

Five things every SDA provider's complaints process should include:

1. A clear intake channel. Complaints should not arrive via personal email, text message, and phone call with no centralised record. Use a single intake point (online form, dedicated email address, or both).

2. Automatic timestamping. The moment a complaint is received, it needs a dated, time-stamped record. Not "sometime last week."

3. Reference numbers. Every complaint gets a unique reference (e.g., CMP-20260214-0001). This ensures nothing gets lost and enables tracking.

4. Countdown tracking. From the moment a complaint is logged, the system should track the acknowledgement deadline (5 days) and resolution deadline (21 days).

5. Escalation triggers. If the acknowledgement deadline is approaching and no action has been taken, someone needs to be alerted. Automatically.

### Tuesday -- Compliance Tips
**Headline**: Your audit log should be tamper-proof. Here is what that actually means.

**Post**:
An audit log records who did what, when, and why in your system. For NDIS compliance, it provides evidence that your records have not been altered after the fact.

But here is the question: how do you prove that the audit log itself has not been tampered with?

If someone can edit or delete audit log entries, the entire log loses its evidentiary value. An auditor cannot rely on a log that might have been modified.

Three levels of audit log integrity:

Level 1: Basic logging (most systems)
Actions are recorded with timestamps and user IDs. But entries can be edited or deleted by system administrators. The log is useful for internal review but has limited evidentiary weight.

Level 2: Immutable logging (best practice)
Entries cannot be deleted or modified after creation. This is a significant improvement but does not prove that entries were not inserted or removed at the database level.

Level 3: Hash-chain integrity (gold standard)
Each audit log entry includes a cryptographic hash that incorporates the hash of the previous entry. Like a blockchain, any insertion, deletion, or modification of an entry breaks the chain. The integrity of the entire log can be verified mathematically.

MySDAManager implements Level 3:
- SHA-256 hash linking between consecutive entries
- Deletion prevention (entries cannot be removed)
- Daily integrity verification via automated cron job (3 AM UTC)
- Verification results included in the Audit Pack Generator (Section 7)

When an auditor reviews your compliance evidence, they can verify that your records have not been altered since creation. That is the difference between "we have records" and "we have provably unmodified records."

**Post**:
There are two approaches to compliance in the SDA sector:

Approach 1: Compliance as a project
Compliance is something you "do" at specific times -- before an audit, after an incident, when a regulation changes. It requires dedicated effort, usually by pulling staff away from their normal responsibilities. The result: periods of high compliance activity followed by long stretches of drift.

Compliance evidence is generated automatically as your team does their normal work. When a maintenance request is logged, the timestamp is the compliance evidence.
